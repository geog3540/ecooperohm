Interpretation of Flow Patterns

1. Places that Attract and Send the Most Flows

The Midwestern states, especially Illinois, Ohio, and Pennsylvania, receive the largest inbound flows, shown by the thickest lines and darkest blue shading. These states were major industrial centers during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, drawing migrants from across the country. In contrast, southern states such as Mississippi and Alabama act as major sending regions, with lighter choropleth shading and numerous outbound flows.

2. General Direction of Flows

The dominant migration patterns move south-to-north and west-to-east. Many flows converge toward the Great Lakes region, reflecting industrial expansion and urban job opportunities. Western states show fewer flows overall, consistent with lower population densities during this period.

3. Relationship Between Flow Patterns, Node Symbols, and Choropleth Map

The choropleth background reinforces the flow patterns: states with high in-migration appear in darker blues and also receive the thickest inbound flows. States with low or negative net migration appear in warmer red tones and send out more flows. The node symbols, placed at state centroids, help anchor the flow lines geographically without distracting from the map’s structure.
